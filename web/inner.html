Like pearls scattered along the long history and connecting chapters that record the interactions between China and the outside world - despite the highs and lows - they are memorable chapters that recount how China opened up and welcomed economic, trade and cultural exchanges with the outside world.

They have witnessed how China became increasingly integrated with the world and promoted the rational, orderly and coordinated development of international immigration in China. They are also valuable experience and wisdom of China’s involvement in the global governance of immigration.

3 STAGES IN 7 DECADES

1949-1978: Searching far and wide: from self-isolation to opening up

Despite the favourable political and economic relations among the Soviet Union, Eastern Europe and China in the 1950s, China remained mostly isolated. It is evident that the under 3% media coverage on regulatory policies reflected our self-isolation and attempts to draw a clear line with the outside world.

1978-2012: The winds of change: the policies of opening up were introduced at different levels

Following China’s opening up, 67% of the coverage on regulatory policies for foreigners in the country increased exponentially. As the scope of China’s opening up continued to expand, covering 92% of the cities and counties, as many as 88 regulatory departments across 20 provinces and municipalities were responsible for the promulgation of regulatory policies for foreigners, showing diverse synergy and complexity in management.

2013-2019: Riding the wind and waves and setting sail: transition from scale-focused development to content-based construction

In 2013, 848,500 foreigners lived in China, and the number showed an annual growth rate of 3.9% for ten consecutive years. Immigration to China was in the spotlight and China began to emerge as an attractive immigration destination.
The intensity of reform was strengthened to ensure rational and controllable development. Meanwhile, the exploration of all-round, in-depth reforms and a coordinated development system supported by big data and active participation in global governance of immigration with Chinese solutions were promoted.

6 CATEGORIES

As China opened up further in an all-round manner, its management of foreigners also transitioned from scale-focused development to being content-based. According to media big data on the management of foreigners in China, six categories cover the full process: from entry and exit and residence to production, investment, business, living, marriage, education, culture, health, sport and entertainment, as well as reward and punishment identical to those of Chinese citizens. Each category is subdivided into 33 detailed items that involve over three-dozen collaborating managing departments.

IMMIGRATION AND ENTRY-EXIT MANAGEMENT / INBOUND TOURISM

1978 Over 100,000 foreign tourists visited China.
1985 The opening up of the Tibetan Plateau saw foreign climbers and explorers flocking to the area.
1994 Foreigners holding a valid visa or residence document may visit Shennongjia National Nature Reserve without applying for a travel document. Procedures for foreign groups in Hong Kong wishing to visit Shenzhen were simplified, and they could stay for 72 hours.

2017 China received over 139.48 million tourists, allowing it to maintain its position as the 4th major tourist destination of the world.
2018 China’s Ministry of Public Security announced the number of countries whose tour groups may visit Hainan Province for up to 30 days without a visa has been raised from 26 to 59, and the policy is also extended from groups to individuals, making it the most practical and open policy in China to date.

2019 The 144-hour visa-free transit was introduced in Beijing, Tianjin, Hebei Province and the Yangtze River Delta, and over 1.8 million visitors took advantage of the policy.

Gender-wise, the number of foreign male tourists is 1.5 times higher than their female counterparts in the past 20 years. However, the growth rate of female visitors is about 1.5 times higher than that of male.

Age-wise, 51% of foreign tourists in 2018 were aged between 25-44, followed by 29% aged between 45-64, with an average growth rate of 5.97 times in 20 years, while those aged between 15-24 increased by 9.38 times, showing the highest jump.

In terms of purpose, the visit of 33% of foreign tourists in 2018 were for sightseeing and leisure, followed by 15% for work and 12% for meetings/business. The highest increase (2001-2018) was 5.72 times for work and 6.42 times for other purposes, showing significant, diverse changes.

The total number of foreign tourists was 5.68 times higher in the past 20 years, and the highest annual growth rate was 1.36 in 2016.

The inbound and outbound tourists in China in the past 20 years increased, respectively, 1.93 and 17.54 times, while the year 2017 witnessed the reversal in inbound- and outbound-tourist numbers.

The foreign-exchange earnings from international tourism in China increased 9 times between 1999-2018, compared with the 18 times increase in domestic tourism.

FOREIGN INVESTMENT AND BUSINESS

The big data of P.D. coverage of this sector, which accounts for 45.8% is a major index that reflects the in-depth participation of foreigners in China’s economic development, including investment, IP protection (invention patent/ publication/ trademark rights and copyright), price control, foreign-exchange/ currency controls and business charges.

The Law of the People’s Republic of China on Chinese-Foreign Equity Joint Ventures in 1979 (the three laws on foreign investment) served as a prelude to foreign investment in China, which offered preferential taxes and simplified the application for business permits. Foreign investment broke the deadlock and began to grow steadily, which, however, concentrated primarily in China’s east-coast area and in small and medium-sized enterprises that were labour intensive and involved in manufacturing.

After China joined the WTO in 1992, foreign investment remained stable and continued to grow. In line with the law of fair competition, China introduced the Enterprise Income Tax Law and revoked preferential taxes for foreign investors. Foreign investors began to focus on quality, which prompted industrial-structure upgrade and expanded to infrastructure construction and new high-tech projects. Meanwhile, systematic protection of IP came into force to protect the legal operations of foreign enterprises in China, including the introduction of the Patent Law, Regulations on the Protection of the Right to Communicate Works over Information Networks and Computers Software Protection.

By the end of 2018, a total of 950,000 foreign companies invested in China, and the actual use of foreign investment exceeded USD2.1 trillion, making foreign direct investment (FDI) a pillar in China’s socioeconomic development.
On 15 March 2019, the 2nd Session of the 13th NPC carried the Foreign Investment Law of the P.R.C. As the first comprehensive and systematic law of China to deal with foreign investment, it marks the country now entering a new stage of regulation-based opening up, which is a milestone in further opening up and the attraction of foreign investment.

MARRIAGE AND FAMILY LIFE

The earliest Provisions for the Registration of Marriage between Chinese Citizens and Foreigners, ratified in 1983. By 2018, among the 10.13 million registered couples across the country, 480,000 involved foreigners, overseas Chinese and residents of Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan.
Policies and regulations concerning foreigners’ marriage and family life in China included marriage registration, adoption, inheritance, medical and health services, religion, entertainment, individual income-tax management and welfare and security.

The revised Regulations on Marriage Registration was promulgated in 2003, a transition from marriage-registration management to service.
Since 2004, the service began keeping diversified and scientific records, including annual statistics of registration of marriage and divorce and the age group and distribution of registrants.
The Adoption Law of the P.R. C (1992), Law of Succession (1985, 2015), Regulation on the Administration of Medical Institutions (1994) Administrative Provisions on Religious Activities of Foreigner Nationals (1990) stipulated the administrative measures for foreigners concerning adoption, inheritance, religious activities or the setting up of medical institutions in China.

CULTURAL EXCHANGE

Cultural exchange has gone from being expansive to being intensive before joining politics and economy as the three major pillars in the country. It comprises education, culture, health, sport and entertainment.
The Regulations on the National Management of Foreign Affairs in 2001 marked a new stage of development in cultural exchange between central and local authorities. The promulgation of the Outline of Education Reform and Development of China (2010) and Study Abroad in China overhauled the admission and management of foreign students in China. By 2020, China will become the top destination for overseas study in Asia.

Strengthening and Improving Cultural Exchanges between China and Foreign Countries (2017) was released, which became the pivotal rules for consolidating Sino-foreign relations and public opinions. The dual forces of going global and attracting foreign resources have integrated cultural exchange into the daily interaction between the Chinese and foreign publics.

China and countries involved in the Belt-and-Road Initiative (BRI) have held cultural years, art festivals, cultural exhibitions and book shows. Cultural heritage collaboration has also been launched with countries such as Indonesia, Myanmar, Serbia and Saudi Arabia, in addition to collaborative projects of archaeology and foreign aid to preserve art facts.

Over the past decade, the annual average number of China’s imported copyrights reached 17,151, with the U.S., the U.K., Japan and Germany being among the top 5 importing countries. The introduction of the Belt-and-Road Initiative saw cultural exchanges and collaboration bloom in China.

In terms of category number, quantity and monetary amount, the import of books, periodicals and newspapers exceeded the export. Between 2008 and 2018, imports dropped to 73% from 81%, while exports increased to 27% from 19%. China’s cultural-exchange policy has gradually developed from solely encouraging imports to reaching out, equal exchanges and mutual learning.

In terms of the category of imported copyrights,by the end of 2018, the import value of books, periodicals and newspapers accounted for 59.6%, 37.36% and 3.03%, respectively, while culture and education, arts and humanities, natural science and children’s books accounted for 39.8% of the total import value.
Over the last decade, all the categories above showed relatively rapid growth rates, exceeding 15 %.

REWARD AND PUNISHMENT

12 new reform policies were launched, which provide services to outstanding foreign youth, students and interns to launch innovative businesses while enhancing the standard of corresponding services and management. Commendations such as the Chinese Government Friendship Award highlight the contribution of foreign experts and friendly individuals to China’s modernisation and economic reform. To date, about 1,500 individuals from over two-dozen countries were presented with the award.

A series of motivational policies including government scholarships and study-in-China scholarships are drawing talents to China. The introduction of certain initiatives ensures foreigners wishing to further their careers in China are able to visit, willing to stay, living comfortably and moving freely. Foreigners are encouraged to actively take part in China’s development, as the country builds an internationally competitive talent-based immigration system.

The coverage of P.D on the administrative policies for the punishment of foreigners include three types of illegal activities (entry, residence, employment), illegal business operations, the Anti-Monopoly Law, illegal acquisition of citizen information (2008)(5), foreign-exchange scam, arbitrage, poaching endangered animals, piracy and rights infringement, smuggling, sale of controlled article, the management of foreign sports coaches and players (qualification verification, transfer, anti-sportsmanship and –ethic behaviour), fire safety, drug trade, scalping cultural artefacts, smuggling wild animals and endangering national security; their coverage accounted for 11.1% of the total coverage.

Ensuring equal legal treatment is also a key indicator of a country’s progressiveness, civility and openness, and no preferential treatment is given based on skin colour or nationality. Anyone who violates the law and regulations shall be treated equally without leniency. Only then will more outstanding foreign talents be drawn to work, live and study in China;
